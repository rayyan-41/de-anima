# AQEEDAH - Orthodox Sunni Critique of Shia Practices

## Introduction: The Dialectic of Orthodoxy and Innovation

The divergence between the Sunni and Shi'i paradigms is not merely a political dispute over historical succession, but a profound theological rupture concerning the nature of divine authority, the integrity of the Prophetic mission, and the boundaries of monotheism (*Tawhid*). This note provides a systematic, scholarly critique of specific Shi'i practices and beliefs from the perspective of Orthodox Sunni theology (*Aqeedah*) and jurisprudence (*Fiqh*), drawing upon the rational and scriptural defenses articulated by classical giants such as Al-Ghazali and Ibn Taymiyyah.

## I. Theological Foundations: Imami Shi'ism vs. Fringe Sects

To comprehend the doctrinal landscape of Shi'a Islam, one must establish a rigorous demarcation between the normative center—represented by Twelver (Ithna 'Ashari) Imami Shi'ism—and the periphery of heterodox sects that diverged into theological extremism. At the heart of this analysis lies the dialectic between preserving the absolute transcendence of God (*Tawhid*) and elevating the spiritual authority of the Prophet's household (*Ahl al-Bayt*). 

The theological foundation of mainstream Imami Shi'ism rests upon two indispensable pillars: *Imamah* (divine leadership) and *Ismah* (infallibility). For the Twelver Shi'a, *Imamah* is not merely a political office but a cosmic necessity. The Earth cannot be left without a *Hujjah* (Proof) of God; otherwise, humanity would be deprived of divine guidance. Thus, the Imams are divinely appointed successors to Prophet Muhammad. However, classical Imami theologians—such as Shaykh al-Mufid, Sharif al-Murtada, and Shaykh al-Tusi—were meticulous in defining the boundaries of this authority. The Imams do not receive new revelation (*Wahy* bringing a new *Shari'ah*), for the seal of prophethood (*Khatm al-Nubuwwah*) culminated irrevocably with Muhammad. Instead, the Imams are the supreme, divinely inspired interpreters of the Qur'anic revelation, possessing comprehensive knowledge of both its exoteric (*Zahir*) and esoteric (*Batin*) dimensions.

Corollary to *Imamah* is the doctrine of *Ismah*. Mainstream Twelvers hold that the Prophets, Fatima al-Zahra, and the Twelve Imams are protected by God from sin, error, and forgetfulness. This absolute infallibility ensures the pristine transmission of religious truth. Yet, it is within the doctrine of *Ismah* that classical Imami scholars established a vital theological firewall: despite their exalted ontological status, the Imams remain categorically human, created beings, and entirely subservient to Allah. They are neither manifestations of the Divine Essence nor partners in God's creative and sustaining acts. To attribute divine qualities to them is to commit the unpardonable sin of polytheism (*Shirk*).

It is precisely at this theological precipice that the fringe sects—collectively categorized in classical heresiography as the *Ghulat* (the extremists or exaggerators)—ruptured from mainstream Islam. The defining characteristic of *Ghuluww* is the transgression of the ontological boundary between the Creator and the created. Historically, the Imams themselves vehemently cursed and excommunicated the *Ghulat*, recognizing them as an existential threat to the purity of Islamic monotheism. The *Ghulat* sects elevated Ali ibn Abi Talib and his descendants from infallible guides to divine hypostases, incarnations, or instantiations of God.

Among the most prominent surviving offshoots rooted in early *Ghuluww* are the Alawites (Nusayris). Tracing their esoteric lineage to Muhammad ibn Nusayr (a follower of the eleventh Imam), the Alawite theology radically departs from normative Shi'ism. Their doctrine is fundamentally syncretic and heavily reliant on an extreme form of *Ta'wil* (esoteric interpretation) that abrogates the exoteric law. At the core of Alawite theology is a divine triad: the *Ma'na* (Essence), represented by Ali; the *Ism* (Name), represented by Muhammad; and the *Bab* (Gate), represented by Salman al-Farisi. In this formulation, Ali is explicitly deified. Furthermore, Alawite cosmology incorporates *Tanasukh* (transmigration of souls), wherein souls undergo successive rebirths—a concept universally condemned by mainstream Islamic eschatology. The antinomianism of the Alawites—their rejection of exoteric rituals like formal prayer and fasting in favor of secret, inner meanings—places them definitively outside the pale of mainstream Imami orthodoxy.

A different divergence is found in Isma'ilism. The schism occurred following the death of the sixth Imam, Ja'far al-Sadiq, when the Isma'ilis recognized his son Isma'il as the seventh Imam. While the early Fatimid Isma'ilis maintained adherence to Islamic jurisprudence, their underlying theology was profoundly influenced by Neoplatonic emanationism. Isma'ili cosmology constructed a vast hierarchy of being, positing God as entirely unknowable, delegating the Prophetic function (*Natiq*, the speaker) and the Imamic function (*Samit*, the silent interpreter) to cosmic intellects. 

The structural danger within Isma'ilism, from a mainstream perspective, lay in its extreme emphasis on the *Batin*. This esoteric trajectory reached its zenith in sub-sects like the Qarmatians and later the Nizari Isma'ilis. In 1164, Nizari leadership proclaimed the *Qiyama* (Great Resurrection), declaring that the spiritual reality of the law had been fully realized, dispensing with the exoteric *Shari'ah* altogether. Though later leaders modified this stance, the precedent of antinomianism remains a hallmark of fringe esotericism. 

In conclusion, the dividing line between mainstream Imami Shi'ism and its fringe offshoots is defined by three unyielding theological parameters: the absolute preservation of *Tawhid* without incarnation; the finality of Prophet Muhammad’s legislative revelation; and the indispensable binding nature of the exoteric *Shari'ah*. Mainstream Twelvers exalt the Imams as infallible arbiters of truth within the strict confines of servanthood (*'ubudiyyah*). Conversely, the *Ghulat*, Alawites, and specific Isma'ili iterations ruptured this equilibrium, descending into deification, reincarnation, and the nullification of Islamic law.

## II. The Doctrine of Imamah and the Status of the Sahabah

The doctrine of *Imamah* (divinely appointed leadership) constitutes the central axis of Shi'i theology, representing the most profound point of departure from Sunni orthodoxy. Whereas Sunni Islam conceptualizes political leadership (*Khilafah*) primarily as a pragmatic and socio-political necessity derived from communal consensus (*Shura*) and historical expediency, Twelver Shi'ism elevates *Imamah* to a fundamental pillar of faith (*Usul al-Din*). This theological elevation dictates that the Imam is not merely a political executive or a scholarly authority, but an infallible (*Ma'sum*) spiritual guide explicitly designated by divine text (*Nass*). This foundational divergence inevitably dictates and dramatically alters the theological assessment of the Prophet Muhammad’s Companions (*Sahabah*), specifically those who assumed leadership immediately following his demise. 

Because the doctrine of *Imamah* posits that 'Ali ibn Abi Talib was explicitly and unequivocally designated as the Prophet’s immediate successor, the historical reality of the caliphates of Abu Bakr, 'Umar, and 'Uthman is viewed within classical Shi'ism not as a legitimate political process, but as a usurpation of a divine mandate. Consequently, the theological principle of *Tabarra*—the obligatory disassociation from the enemies of God, the Prophet, and the *Ahl al-Bayt* (the Prophet's family)—became inextricably linked to the historical events of the succession. *Tabarra* transitioned from a generalized principle of spiritual allegiance into a retrospective political and theological judgment. In its more stringent historical articulations, this principle necessitated the explicit condemnation, anathematization (*Takfir*), and ritualistic cursing (*Sabb*) of the first three Caliphs, with particular vitriol directed toward Abu Bakr and 'Umar, often referred to pejoratively in polemical literature. The underlying theological logic is stark: if the Prophet's command regarding 'Ali was explicit, public, and binding, the failure of the general body of the *Sahabah* to enforce his succession represents either a mass apostasy, a grand conspiracy of silence, or an unprecedented collective disobedience. Therefore, the veneration of the *Ahl al-Bayt* (*Tawalla*) was deemed incomplete without the active denunciation of those perceived to have usurped their cosmic right.

The classical Orthodox Sunni critique of this paradigm is multifaceted, rooting itself in both scriptural hermeneutics and historical pragmatism. Sunni orthodoxy is predicated upon the explicit Quranic attestation of the Companions' collective probity, righteousness, and divine pleasure (*Radiyallahu 'anhum*). The Sunni methodology asserts that while the individual *Sahabah* were not ontologically infallible—and were therefore capable of political miscalculations, sins, and errors in judgment—they were collectively preserved from agreeing upon a fundamental deviation that would corrupt the religion. The Sunni paradigm views the anathema of the Companions as an epistemological catastrophe. To suggest that the overwhelming majority of the Companions—the very individuals who fought alongside the Prophet, memorized the Quran, witnessed the contexts of revelation, and preserved the Prophetic *Sunnah*—conspired to conceal a divine mandate fundamentally undermines the transmission of the entirety of the Islamic tradition.

This defense is most rigorously systematized in the polemical works of Taqi al-Din Ibn Taymiyyah, particularly in his magnum opus, *Minhaj al-Sunnah an-Nabawiyyah* (The Methodology of the Prophetic Sunnah). Ibn Taymiyyah provides an exhaustive, rationalist, and scriptural refutation of the Shi'i narrative. He argues that the premise of a hidden or suppressed *Nass* contradicts the very nature of the Prophetic mission, which was defined by clear, unambiguous, and public transmission (*al-Balagh al-Mubin*). Ibn Taymiyyah meticulously deconstructs the vilification of Abu Bakr and 'Umar by highlighting their unparalleled historical, material, and spiritual sacrifices during the formative and most vulnerable years of Islam. He argues that their foundational role in establishing and defending the faith makes their subsequent anathematization a logical absurdity. For Ibn Taymiyyah, cursing the architects of the Islamic state is tantamount to declaring the absolute failure of the Prophet's primary mission: the cultivation of a righteous, God-fearing community. He posits that if the \"best of generations\" could so easily betray the Prophet's core directive immediately upon his death, the efficacy of his prophethood itself is implicitly called into question. 

Complementing Ibn Taymiyyah’s rigorous historical polemics is the profound theological and spiritual defense articulated by Abu Hamid al-Ghazali. Approaching the issue through the dual lenses of legal theory (*Usul al-Fiqh*) and spiritual propriety (*Tasawwuf*), al-Ghazali addresses the status of the *Sahabah* in major works such as *Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din* (The Revival of the Religious Sciences) and *Fada'ih al-Batiniyya* (The Infamies of the Esoterics). Al-Ghazali champions the orthodox position of *Imsak 'an Ma Shajara Baynahum*—the strict imperative of withholding the tongue from delving into the historical disputes, civil strife, and political maneuverings that occurred among the Companions. Al-Ghazali argues that the cursing of any Muslim is a severe spiritual contagion that hardens the heart, and directing such vitriol toward the most eminent Companions is spiritually ruinous. He maintains that their immense virtues, their sacrifices, and their ultimate salvation are established by definitive, mass-transmitted texts (*Mutawatir*), and that historical ambiguities, political nuances, or isolated historical reports (*Ahad*) cannot possibly abrogate definitive scriptural praise.

For al-Ghazali, the defense of the *Sahabah* transcends mere historical apologetics; it is an absolute necessity for the survival of Islamic jurisprudence and theology. Invalidating the moral integrity of the Companions collapses the chain of transmission (*Sanad*) upon which the entire edifice of the religion rests. If the first generation is deemed collectively unreliable, politically compromised, or treacherous, the authenticity of the Quranic compilation and the vast corpus of Hadith is fatally nullified. Thus, al-Ghazali frames the veneration of the *Sahabah* not as a naive denial of their human fallibility, but as a theological acknowledgment of their indispensable role as the faithful conduits of the final revelation, asserting that their collective integrity is eternally guaranteed by the very God who promised to preserve the religion.

## III. Istighathah and the Invocation of 'Ya Ali Madad'

The practice of *Istighathah*—conceptually defined as seeking emergency aid, rescue from distress, or supernatural intervention—occupies a profoundly contentious space within Islamic theology. When directed exclusively towards the Divine, it represents the highest form of reliance and worship, an acknowledgment of human fragility and Allah's absolute omnipotence. However, when directed towards deceased creation, such as the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ), the Ahl al-Bayt, or righteous saints (*Awliya*), it raises severe theological alarms within Orthodox Sunni Islam. The specific invocation \"Ya Ali Madad\" (O Ali, help me) serves as a quintessential paradigm of this practice. Prevalent primarily within Shi'i and certain esoteric Sufi traditions, the phrase is uttered with the underlying ontological belief that the deceased Imam possesses a continuous, active spiritual presence and the intermediary agency to respond to the petitioner's worldly and spiritual needs.

To analyze this invocation from an Orthodox Sunni perspective, one must first delineate the foundational paradigm of *Tawhid* (Islamic monotheism), specifically *Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah* (the Oneness of Worship) and *Tawhid al-Asma wa al-Sifat* (the Oneness of Divine Names and Attributes). In the Sunni creed, the act of supplication (*Du'a*) is inextricably synonymous with worship (*Ibadah*). This is firmly grounded in the prophetic tradition, \"Supplication is the essence of worship,\" and the explicit Qur'anic injunction: \"And your Lord says, 'Call upon Me; I will respond to you.' Indeed, those who disdain My worship will enter Hell rendered contemptible\" (Qur'an 40:60). Consequently, directing any form of worship, including the plea for unseen, supernatural aid, to an entity other than Allah constitutes a foundational breach of the monotheistic covenant.

A critical analytical step is distinguishing between *Tawassul* (seeking a legitimate means of nearness to Allah) and *Istighathah*. Orthodox Sunni theology, as articulated by the consensus of classical scholars, permits specific, textually validated forms of *Tawassul*. Legitimate means include invoking Allah by His Beautiful Names, approaching Him through the merit of one's own righteous deeds, or requesting a living, physically present righteous person to supplicate on one's behalf. However, *Istighathah* directed at the deceased represents a categorically different and perilous ontological claim. Asking a deceased saint or Imam to fulfill a need directly—or even imploring them to act as a cosmic courier to ask Allah on the petitioner's behalf from within the *Barzakh* (the intermediary realm of the dead)—assumes they possess attributes bordering on the Divine.

Taqi al-Din Ahmad Ibn Taymiyyah (d. 728 AH) provides one of the most rigorous and historically influential critiques of this practice in works such as *Qa'idah Jalilah fi al-Tawassul wa al-Wasilah*. Ibn Taymiyyah meticulously deconstructs the mechanics of intercession (*Shafa'ah*) and unseen invocation. He argues that calling upon the dead, who are absent from the physical realm of cause and effect, implicitly assigns to them the divine attributes of omnipresence (the ability to hear millions of simultaneous prayers globally across different languages) and independent supernatural agency. Ibn Taymiyyah posits that while the deceased, including the prophets, are \"alive with their Lord\" in a spiritual sense, the barrier of the *Barzakh* completely severs their interaction with the material world's mechanics. The classical debate regarding the \"hearing of the dead\" (*Sama' al-Mawta*) concludes that even if the dead can hear specific things at specific times by Allah's will, they are entirely bereft of the capacity to respond, intercede autonomously, or alter worldly outcomes. 

Therefore, uttering \"Ya Ali Madad\" crosses the theological demarcation from permissible reverence into the territory of *Shirk* (polytheism). The severity of this transgression is categorized by the exact conviction of the supplicant. If the supplicant believes that Ali ibn Abi Talib possesses independent power to grant the request, this is undeniably *Shirk Akbar* (major polytheism), as it violates *Tawhid al-Rububiyyah* (Oneness of Lordship) by granting a creation a share in the divine governance of the universe. Alternatively, if the supplicant believes Ali acts merely as an intermediary but still directs the prayer directly to him rather than to Allah, it remains a severe violation of *Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah*, as the action of the prayer itself is diverted from the Creator to the created. 

The Sunni Orthodox framework maintains that the metaphysical architecture of Islam is inherently egalitarian and direct; it vehemently abhors the establishment of a clerical or saintly pantheon that mediates salvation or divine assistance. The invocation of \"Ya Ali Madad\" implicitly constructs a hierarchy of intermediaries, echoing the sociological and theological practices of pre-Islamic Arabia. The Meccan polytheists justified their idols not as independent gods, but as lesser intercessors who would bring them closer to the Supreme Deity: \"We only worship them that they may bring us nearer to Allah in position\" (Qur'an 39:3). By drawing this striking parallel, classical Sunni scholars argue that the mechanization of intercession through the deceased replicates the very theological error the Qur'anic revelation sought to eradicate. 

Ultimately, the boundary line in Orthodox Sunni theology rests entirely on the exclusivity of unseen reliance. While the profound love, respect, and emulation of Ali ibn Abi Talib and the Ahl al-Bayt are mandatory components of Sunni faith, elevating their status to cosmic intercessors capable of responding to *Istighathah* dismantles strict monotheism. The cry for help in the unseen realm is the exclusive prerogative of the Sustainer of the Worlds, rendering invocations like \"Ya Ali Madad\" incompatible with the pristine, unmediated Tawhid championed by classical Sunni orthodoxy.

## IV. Ritual Mourning and Tatbir (Self-Flagellation)

**1. Historical Evolution of Ashura Mourning: From Lamentation to Physical Liturgy**
The development of ritualized mourning for Imam al-Hussain (r.a.) represents a complex layering of historical trauma, political institutionalization, and regional cultural syncretism. Historically, the initial responses to the Tragedy of Karbala (61 AH) were characterized by the internal, psychological grief of the immediate survivors and the subsequent poetic lamentations of the *Tawwabun* (Penitents). These early expressions focused on the moral failure of the Ummah and the theological significance of the Imam's sacrifice. However, the metamorphosis of these personal expressions into public, state-sponsored processions was initiated during the Buyid Dynasty in the 4th Century AH. Under the Buyid emirs, who were of Daylamite origin and Shi'ite inclination, the 10th of Muharram was formally declared a day of public mourning, seeing the closure of markets and the introduction of public lamentation circles (*Ma’tam*).

The introduction of physical implements—specifically *Zanjir-zani* (the use of chains) and *Tatbir/Qama-zani* (the striking of the head with swords to draw blood)—is a significantly later development that lacks any foundation in the early *Maqtal* (martyrdom narratives) or the formative centuries of Islamic history. Rather, these practices emerged as a dominant liturgical feature during the Safavid and Qajar eras in Persia (16th–19th Centuries). Scholarly consensus identifies these as a synthesis of regional cultural traditions—possibly influenced by Caucasian Christian passion plays or Central Asian shamanistic practices—that were retrospectively integrated into the religious identity of the masses. In the contemporary era, these acts have become a central pillar of Ashura identity in specific geographical regions, particularly in Iraq, Iran, and the South Asian subcontinent, where they serve as a visceral, visual display of loyalty to the Ahl al-Bayt through the shared shedding of blood.

**2. Jurisprudential (Fiqh) Critique: The Prohibition of Self-Harm and the Amanah of the Body**
From the perspective of orthodox Sunni jurisprudence (*Fiqh*), the fundamental legal barrier to *Tatbir* and *Zanjir* is the absolute prohibition of self-inflicted harm (*Idrar bi-nafs*). The Shari’ah is underpinned by the *Maqasid* (Objectives) of preserving five core elements: religion, life, intellect, progeny, and property. The preservation of life (*Hifdh al-Nafs*) is a paramount legal obligation, and any ritual that endangers the physical integrity of the believer is prima facie invalid. The foundational legal maxim *La darar wa la dirar* (\"There shall be no harm nor reciprocating of harm\") serves as an overarching constraint on any action that compromises the health or safety of the person.

In the Sunni paradigm, the human body is not the property of the individual to be disposed of through ritualized mutilation; rather, it is an *Amanah* (Trust) from Allah. To intentionally inflict wounds, cause profuse bleeding, or risk permanent infection or scarring for the purpose of ritual catharsis constitutes a violation of this divine trust. While some contemporary proponents argue that such acts are a form of 'sacrifice' or 'solidarity,' the four major Sunni schools of law (*Madhahib*) maintain that an act cannot be a legitimate form of worship if it violates a primary prohibition of the Shari'ah. The Shafi'i, Hanafi, Maliki, and Hanbali jurists are unanimous in their condemnation of self-mutilation. Consequently, the majority of Sunni *Fuqaha* (Jurists) categorize these practices not merely as *Makruh* (Disliked), but as *Haram* (Prohibited), as they involve the intentional destruction of the physical form without a valid Shari’i necessity (*Darura*). This is consistent with the Quranic command: \"And do not throw [yourselves] with your [own] hands into destruction\" (Surah al-Baqarah, 2:195).

**3. The Theological Definition of Bid’ah (Innovation) in Ritual Worship**
The theological critique of self-flagellation centers on the critical distinction between *Deen* (revealed religion) and cultural invention. In the orthodox Sunni framework, ritual worship (*Ibadah*) is *tawqifi*—meaning it is strictly limited to what has been explicitly legislated by Allah and modeled by His Messenger (s.a.w.). The introduction of elaborate, physically violent rituals into the commemoration of a religious event is viewed as a clear departure from the Sunnah. 

Bid’ah (innovation) in religion is defined as an invented way in religion that mimics the Shari’ah and is intended to be practiced as an act of devotion, but lacks a legitimate basis in the Quran or the Sunnah of the Prophet (s.a.w.) and the Rightly Guided Caliphs (*Khulafa al-Rashidun*). By elevating self-flagellation to a pseudo-sacramental status, practitioners risk creating a parallel system of worship that was entirely unknown to the first generations of Muslims (*Salaf al-Salih*). The Prophet (s.a.w.) explicitly warned: \"Whoever innovates in this matter of ours what is not part of it, it will be rejected\" (Bukhari). The absence of these practices among the Sahaba (r.a.) and the early Ahl al-Bayt—including Imam Zain al-Abidin (r.a.) and the early Imams—is a decisive theological proof that they do not constitute a part of the original Islamic practice. The 'perpetual trauma' model of mourning is thus seen as a later addition that distorts the prophetic message of hope and divine decree.

**4. Scriptural Evidence: The Prohibition of the 'Cry of Jahiliyyah'**
The most potent scriptural evidence against these practices lies in the explicit Prophetic prohibitions regarding mourning customs. The Messenger of Allah (s.a.w.) stated: \"He is not of us who strikes his cheeks, tears his clothes, and calls with the cry of the Ignorance (*Da'wa al-Jahiliyyah*)\" (Bukhari, Muslim). This Hadith targets the externalization of grief through physical violence and vocal hysteria. The striking of the face (*Latm*), the tearing of garments, and the wailing (*Niyaha*) were condemned by the Prophet (s.a.w.) as denials of *Rida bi-Qada* (Contentment with the Divine Decree). 

If the Prophet (s.a.w.) forbade simple cheek-slapping and the tearing of one's collar—actions that do not typically result in bloodshed—then, by the principle of *A Fortiori* (*Min Bab al-Awla*), the cutting of the scalp with blades and the scourging of the back with chains are even more severely condemned. These acts are viewed as an intensification of the very *Jahiliyyah* behaviors the Prophet (s.a.w.) sought to eradicate. Furthermore, another Hadith states: \"Indeed, the deceased is punished in his grave by the wailing of his family over him\" (Bukhari). This suggests that exaggerated mourning rituals, rather than benefiting the soul of the deceased, represent a spiritual burden and a departure from the prophetic guidance of *Sabr al-Jamil* (Beautiful Patience). The focus on physical pain is a reversal of the Islamic shift from external ritualism to internal spiritual transformation.
