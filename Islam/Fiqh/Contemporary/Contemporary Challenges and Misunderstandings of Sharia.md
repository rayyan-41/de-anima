# FIQH - Contemporary Challenges and Misunderstandings of Sharia

- - -

## Section 1: Deen vs. Culture: A Methodology for Reclaiming the Sharia

The primary crisis in contemporary Islamic thought is the failure to distinguish between the *Deen*—the immutable, divine core of Islam—and *Culture*—the historically contingent and geographically localized expressions of that core. This failure is particularly acute in South Asian contexts, where centuries of localized practice have often been conflated with the sacred Law (Sharia). Imam Al-Ghazali, in his *Mustasfa* and *Ihya*, provides the necessary epistemological framework to untangle this knot. His methodology was one of radical skepticism toward "inherited practice" that lacked a direct, verifiable link to the *Maqasid* (Objectives) of the Law.

Culture often masks its origins as divine law through the mechanism of *Urf* (Custom). While *Urf* is a valid source of law in the four madhabs, it is subordinate to the *Nass* (Text) and the *Maqasid*. When a cultural practice—such as the exclusion of women from inheritance or the prioritization of tribal honor over individual justice—becomes entrenched, it is often back-filled with weak or fabricated narrations to give it an Islamic veneer. Al-Ghazali’s anti-dogmatic approach insists that we return to the first principles of justice (*Adl*) and mercy (*Rahmah*). If a ruling derived from custom violates these principles, it is not Sharia; it is merely an ossified social habit.

Scholars like **Akram Nadwi** and **Wael Hallaq** have noted that the "Blue Wall" of traditionalism often protects cultural biases rather than religious truths. By reclaiming the Ghazalian methodology, we recognize that Sharia is not a static code frozen in 10th-century Baghdad or 18th-century Delhi, but a living, breathing application of divine justice to human reality. Reclaiming Sharia, therefore, requires a surgical removal of the cultural barnacles that have attached themselves to the hull of the Deen.

## Section 2: The Political Origins of Apostasy (Riddah): Historical Realism vs. Theological Dogma

The contemporary criticism of Islam's "death penalty for apostasy" almost universally fails to account for the political reality of the 7th-century Arabian state. In the classical period, the Islamic community (*Ummah*) was not merely a religious group but a nascent political entity in a state of constant existential war. Apostasy (*Riddah*) during this time was synonymous with **Treason** (*Baghy*). To leave the faith was to physically defect to the enemy, carrying military secrets and tribal allegiances with one.

The *Hurub al-Riddah* (Apostasy Wars) under Caliph Abu Bakr were not launched because individuals changed their theological beliefs in private, but because tribal leaders refused to recognize the central authority of Medina, withheld the Zakat (the state tax), and mobilized armies to destroy the early Muslim state. As noted by **Taha Jabir al-Alwani**, the Quran itself contains over 20 verses regarding apostasy, yet not one of them prescribes a worldly punishment. The punishment mentioned is always in the hereafter.

### The Distinction Between Baghy and Riddah
The confusion in later centuries arose from the linguistic overlap between theological exit from Islam and political rebellion. In the Prophetic era, these were one and the same. To leave the religion was to join the enemies of the state (*Muharib*). However, as the Islamic state stabilized, the two concepts began to diverge. Scholars like **Ibn al-Humam** (Hanafi) argued that the punishment for the *Murtad* (Apostate) was contingent upon their *Muharabah* (active hostility). If an individual merely changed their belief but remained a peaceful citizen, the state had no grounds for execution.

This is supported by the practice of the early community. **Ibrahim al-Nakha'i**, a leading scholar of the Tabi'un, argued that the apostate should be invited to repent indefinitely, effectively negating the death penalty. The modern application of *Riddah* laws to suppress intellectual dissent or personal conversion is a gross distortion of the original juridical intent, which was focused solely on the preservation of the state's security during a time of total war.

## Section 3: Divergence of the Four Madhabs on Riddah: From Treason to Intellectual Dissent

The four major madhabs demonstrates a significant range of opinion on the mechanics of *Riddah*. The Hanafis, for instance, famously ruled that a woman could not be executed for apostasy, as she was generally not a combatant in the political wars of the time. This further proves that the core of the ruling was combat-readiness and treason, not the act of unbelief itself.

The Shafi'is and Malikis emphasized the *Istitaabah* (the period of seeking repentance). While some insisted on a three-day window, others argued that the period for seeking repentance should last as long as there is hope for a return to the community—effectively indefinitely. This shift from a swift judicial execution to a prolonged intellectual dialogue shows that the early jurists were more concerned with the preservation of the soul and the stability of the state than with the "thought police" tactics of modern extremists.

Contemporary scholars like **Yusuf al-Qaradawi** have distinguished between "Major Apostasy" (which involves active subversion of the state) and "Minor Apostasy" (a private shift in belief). The latter, he argued, is a matter between the individual and God. The divergence within the madhabs serves as a mercy, providing the intellectual tools to navigate a world where the medieval "House of Islam" vs. "House of War" paradigm no longer exists.

## Section 4: Zina vs. Hirabah: A Jurisprudential Distinction in Cases of Sexual Assault

One of the most egregious misunderstandings of Sharia is the conflation of **Zina** (consensual adultery/fornication) with **Hirabah** (rape/armed assault). In many cultural contexts, victims of rape are told they need four witnesses to prove the crime, and if they fail, they are charged with *Zina* themselves. This is a perversion of the Law that has no basis in the primary texts.

*Zina* is a "Crime against God's limits" (*Hadd*) and carries a high evidentiary bar (four eyewitnesses to the act of penetration) precisely to **protect** people from false accusations (*Qadhf*). It is a law designed to prevent the state from entering the private bedrooms of citizens. **Rape**, however, is not *Zina*; it is an act of **Hirabah** (war against society) or **Ikrah** (compulsion). It is a violent assault where the victim is an innocent party.

### The Legal Framework for Assault
In cases of *Hirabah*, the evidentiary standards are entirely different. The victim is not an "accuser" in a *Zina* sense but a complainant in a criminal assault. As **Ibn al-Qayyim** argued in *At-Turuq al-Hukmiyya*, the judge is obligated to use every available means—including forensic evidence, medical reports, and circumstantial details—to establish the truth and protect the innocent. To require a rape victim to produce four witnesses is not only a logistical impossibility but a juridical absurdity that violates the *Maqasid* of justice.

## Section 5: Evidence in Rape Cases: Beyond the 'Four Witnesses' Misconception

The requirement of four witnesses is exclusive to the specific charge of *Zina*. In all other areas of Sharia, including assault, theft, and murder, the standard is two witnesses—and even then, circumstantial evidence (*Qarinah*) is often sufficient for a conviction. In cases of rape, the "four witnesses" rule is a cultural invention used to silence women and protect perpetrators.

Early jurists, including members of the **Maliki** school, recognized the validity of circumstantial evidence. If a woman emerged from a house crying for help, disheveled and injured, and identified a man who was seen fleeing the scene, this constitutes a powerful *Qarinah*. Under the principle of *Siyasa Shari'iyya* (Just Governance), a ruler or judge can impose *Ta'zir* (discretionary) punishments based entirely on forensic and circumstantial evidence.

Modern scholars like **Mohammad Akram Nadwi** have emphasized that the Sharia's primary goal is the "Prevention of Harm" (*Dar' al-Mafasid*). A system that allows a rapist to go free because of a technicality meant for a different category of crime is, by definition, un-Islamic. The "four witnesses" argument is a weapon of the patriarchy, not a tool of the Sharia.

## Section 6: The Quranic Witness (2:282): Financial Testimony vs. Judicial Equivalence

Contemporary critics often point to Surah Al-Baqarah 2:282—which mentions having "two men or a man and two women" as witnesses for a debt contract—as evidence that Islam considers a woman's testimony to be worth half that of a man. This is a gross misreading of the verse's context and its application in the broader legal system.

The verse specifically pertains to **commercial transactions** and **debt contracts** in a 7th-century context where women were generally not involved in the public marketplace. The provision of a second woman was not because of a lack of "intellect" but to provide **support** (*dhikr*) in an environment that was foreign to them. This was a procedural safeguard, not an ontological statement on the value of a woman's soul.

### Judicial Equality
In fact, there are numerous areas of Sharia where a woman's testimony is not only equal but **superior** or **exclusive**. In matters related to childbirth, nursing, and specific female medical conditions, the testimony of a single woman can override that of multiple men. As **Imam al-Qarafi** (Maliki) noted, testimony (*Shahadah*) is based on *Khabar* (information) and *Dabt* (accuracy). Where a person is most knowledgeable, their testimony carries the most weight.

## Section 7: Women's Testimony in Criminal and Personal Law: A Comparative Analysis

Beyond the specific case of debt contracts, the vast majority of Islamic jurisprudence treats the testimony of men and women as equivalent. In the transmission of **Hadith**—the second most important source of Law—the testimony of a woman is identical to that of a man. Aishah (RA), the Prophet's wife, is one of the most prolific narrators of Hadith, and her word on the most sacred matters of the Deen was never "halved."

In criminal cases (*Hudud* and *Qisas*), there is a scholarly debate. Some classical jurists restricted testimony to men, but others, like **Ibn Hazm** (Zahiri) and several scholars from the **Hanbali** and **Maliki** schools, argued that women's testimony is valid in all cases, including those involving life and limb. The restriction in some schools was a "precautionary" measure (*Ihtiyat*) to spare women the trauma of the brutal evidentiary process.

Modern legal systems that halve women's testimony across the board are following a distorted version of the Law. Scholars like **Akram Nadwi** have demonstrated through their research on female scholars (*Muhaddithat*) that women were the intellectual equals and teachers of the great male jurists throughout Islamic history.

## Section 8: Case Studies and Historical Precedents: Justice in Practice

Historical records from the Ottoman courts and the early Caliphate demonstrate a high degree of pragmatism and justice that is often missing from modern "Sharia" courts. For instance, the use of forensic evidence (such as examining bloodstains or garments) was a common feature of Maliki and Hanafi jurisprudence long before the advent of modern forensics.

In one famous case, a woman accused a man of rape, and the man claimed it was consensual *Zina*. The judge used circumstantial evidence—the woman's immediate report and her physical state—to convict the man of assault (*Ikrah*), despite the absence of four witnesses. This shows that the classical masters understood the spirit of the Law far better than the literalists of today.

## Section 9: Conclusion: Reclaiming the Ethical Spirit of the Sharia

The Sharia is not a static monolith; it is an ethical framework designed to realize the "Common Good" (*Maslaha*). When the application of a law results in manifest injustice—such as the execution of an intellectual for a change of heart or the silencing of a rape victim—it is the duty of the jurist to revisit the *Nass* and the *Maqasid*. As Imam Al-Ghazali famously stated, the Law exists for the benefit of humanity, not the other way around.

The contemporary criticisms of Islam often target the cultural barnacles that have choked the true spirit of the Deen. By utilizing an anti-dogmatic approach and returning to the high-fidelity synthesis of the classical masters, we can reclaim a Sharia that is synonymous with justice, mercy, and human dignity.

The separation of *Deen* from *Culture* is the most urgent task of our time. We must refuse the "inherited practices" of the recent past and return to the radical, liberating potential of the original message.
