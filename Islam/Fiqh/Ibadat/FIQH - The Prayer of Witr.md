# FIQH - The Prayer of Witr: Rulings, Methodologies, and Variations Across the Four Madhabs

## Introduction: The Linguistic and Shari'ah Meaning of Witr
The word *Witr* (or *Watr*, depending on the Arabic dialect) linguistically translates to "odd" as opposed to "even" (*Shaf'*). In the context of Islamic jurisprudence (Fiqh), *Salat al-Witr* refers to the final prayer performed at night, consisting of an odd number of units (Rak'ahs), which serves as a seal upon the night prayers. 

The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) placed immense emphasis on the Witr prayer, stating: *"Allah is Witr (One) and loves the Witr, so perform the Witr, O people of the Quran!"* (Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi). Due to the strong prophetic emphasis and the variety of authentic narrations detailing how the Prophet (ﷺ) performed it, the four Sunni schools of jurisprudence—Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali—have developed distinct, albeit harmonious, methodologies regarding its legal status, structure, and timing. 

This note provides a comprehensive four-madhab breakdown of the rulings pertaining to Witr, highlighting the points of agreement, contention, and the underlying textual evidences that form the basis for each school's position.

## The Legal Status (Hukm) of Witr: Wajib vs. Sunnah Mu'akkadah

The foremost point of divergence among the scholars regarding the Witr prayer is its legal classification. While all agree on its immense importance, they differ on whether it is an absolute obligation.

### The Position of the Majority (Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali): Sunnah Mu'akkadah
The Jumhur (majority) of scholars classify Witr as a *Sunnah Mu'akkadah* (a highly emphasized recommended act) rather than a *Fard* or *Wajib* (obligation). 

**Evidence for the Majority:**
1. **The Hadith of the Bedouin:** When a bedouin asked the Prophet (ﷺ) about the obligatory prayers, he replied, "Five prayers in a day and a night." The man asked, "Is there anything else upon me?" The Prophet replied, "No, unless you do so voluntarily" (Bukhari and Muslim).
2. **Statement of Ali (RA):** Ali ibn Abi Talib said, "The Witr is not an absolute obligation like your prescribed prayers, but it is a Sunnah established by the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ)" (Tirmidhi).

The Hanbalis emphasize it so strongly that Imam Ahmad famously stated regarding the one who habitually abandons Witr: *"He is a bad man; his testimony should not be accepted."*

### The Position of the Hanafi School: Wajib (Necessary)
The Hanafi school uniquely distinguishes between *Fard* (obligatory by definitive proof, i.e., Quran/Mutawatir Hadith) and *Wajib* (necessary by probable proof, i.e., Ahad Hadith). They classify Witr as **Wajib**.

**Evidence for the Hanafis:**
1. **Imperative Commands:** The Prophet (ﷺ) used the command form: *"Witr is a duty upon every Muslim..."* (Abu Dawud, though graded weak by some, Hanafis accept its corroborating chains).
2. **Another Hadith:** *"Allah has added another prayer for you, which is Witr, so pray it between the Isha prayer and the dawn"* (Ahmad). The use of the word "added" implies necessity.
3. Consequently, in the Hanafi school, if a person misses Witr, they are obliged to make it up (*Qada*), whereas for the majority, making it up is highly recommended but not a strict legal debt.

## The Number of Rak'ahs and the Method of Performance

Because the Prophet (ﷺ) prayed Witr in various formats throughout his life to demonstrate permissibility, the schools adopted different formats as their preferred standard.

### The Maliki School
**Number:** Primarily 1 Rak'ah, preceded by 2 Rak'ahs of *Shaf'* (even).
**Methodology:** The Malikis heavily prefer separating the Witr from the preceding voluntary night prayers. One prays two Rak'ahs (the *Shaf'*), performs the *Taslim* (salaam to exit the prayer), and then stands to pray exactly one single Rak'ah as Witr.
**Evidence:** Ibn Umar reported that the Prophet (ﷺ) used to separate the Shaf' and the Witr with a Taslim, speaking to them in between (Bukhari). For Malikis, praying three Rak'ahs connected like Maghrib is highly disliked (Makruh) because of the Prophet's explicit prohibition: *"Do not make Witr resemble Maghrib."*

### The Shafi'i and Hanbali Schools
**Number:** Minimum of 1 Rak'ah; normally 3; maximum of 11.
**Methodology:** They offer immense flexibility based on the various narrations.
1. **Separation (Fasl):** Praying 2 Rak'ahs, doing Taslim, then praying 1 Rak'ah. This is the **preferred** method for both Shafi'is and Hanbalis.
2. **Connection (Wasl):** Praying 3 Rak'ahs continuously with only *one* Tashahhud at the very end. This avoids resembling Maghrib (which has two Tashahhuds).
3. Praying 5, 7, or 9 continuously with one Tashahhud at the end is also valid and practiced based on authentic narrations in Sahih Muslim.

### The Hanafi School
**Number:** Exactly 3 Rak'ahs.
**Methodology:** The Hanafis strictly hold that Witr is three continuous Rak'ahs with two Tashahhuds—structurally identical to the Maghrib prayer. They do not permit praying a single solitary Rak'ah, arguing that no prayer consists of less than two Rak'ahs except for Witr when joined with the preceding ones.
**Evidence:** 
1. A'isha (RA) reported that the Prophet (ﷺ) would not say the Taslim after the first two Rak'ahs of Witr (Nasai).
2. Regarding the prohibition of making it look like Maghrib, the Hanafis interpret this as a prohibition of leaving out the *Qunut* supplication, which differentiates it from Maghrib.

## The Qunut in Witr: Rulings, Timing, and Raising the Hands

*Qunut* is a supplication made while standing during the prayer. Its application in Witr is a major point of comparative Fiqh.

### The Hanafi Position
*   **Ruling:** Wajib (necessary).
*   **Timing:** All year round.
*   **Position:** In the third Rak'ah, **before** Ruku'. The worshipper recites the Fatiha and a Surah, says *Allahu Akbar*, raises the hands to the ears (Takbeer of Tahrimah), folds them again, and recites the Qunut silently.
*   **Specific Dua:** The preferred Dua begins with *"Allahumma inna nasta'inuka..."*

### The Shafi'i Position
*   **Ruling:** Sunnah.
*   **Timing:** Only during the **last half of Ramadan**. (Though they perform Qunut in the Fajr prayer year-round).
*   **Position:** **After** rising from Ruku'.
*   **Specific Dua:** The preferred Dua is the one taught to Hasan ibn Ali (RA): *"Allahumma-hdini fiman hadayt..."* spoken aloud.

### The Hanbali Position
*   **Ruling:** Sunnah.
*   **Timing:** All year round.
*   **Position:** **After** rising from Ruku', though before Ruku' is valid. The hands are raised in supplication, and the face is wiped afterward (though wiping the face is disputed within the school).

### The Maliki Position
*   **Ruling:** Disliked (Makruh) to perform Qunut in Witr.
*   **Timing:** Never in Witr. The Malikis strictly reserve the Qunut for the Fajr prayer, where it is done silently before Ruku'.

## The Timing of Witr

There is total consensus (Ijma) among the four schools regarding the time frame for Witr.

*   **Beginning:** Immediately after the Isha prayer is performed (even if combined early with Maghrib due to travel/rain).
*   **End:** The emergence of the true dawn (Fajr/Subh al-Sadiq).

**When is it best to pray?**
The Prophet (ﷺ) said: *"Whoever fears that he will not wake up in the latter part of the night, let him pray Witr in the first part of it; and whoever hopes to wake up in the latter part of it, let him pray it at the end of the night, for the prayer at the end of the night is witnessed, and that is better"* (Muslim).
Thus, all four schools agree that delaying Witr to the last third of the night is superior for the one confident in waking up. However, during Ramadan, praying it in congregation after Taraweeh is considered superior to praying it alone at the end of the night.

## Making Up a Missed Witr (Qada)

What happens if a person sleeps through the Witr prayer and wakes up after Fajr has begun?

*   **Hanafi:** Because it is *Wajib*, it must be made up as a debt. It is made up as 3 Rak'ahs exactly as it would have been prayed at night.
*   **Shafi'i & Hanbali:** It is highly recommended to make it up during the daytime (between Ishraq and Dhuhr). However, the Prophet (ﷺ) would make it up by praying an *even* number. A'isha (RA) reported that if sleep or pain overtook him, he would pray 12 Rak'ahs during the day (Muslim). Thus, if one usually prays 3, they make up 4.
*   **Maliki:** If dawn breaks and one has not prayed Witr, they must pray Fajr. However, if they wake up *just* as dawn is breaking (Fajr time has entered) but haven't prayed the Fard of Fajr, they can still quickly pray the Witr as long as they don't fear missing the Fajr prayer entirely before sunrise. Once Fajr is prayed, Witr is not made up.

## References and Authentication

1. **Al-Muwatta** by Imam Malik: For the Maliki strict reliance on separating the Shaf' and Witr (Amal of Madinah) and the omission of Qunut in Witr.
2. **Al-Umm** by Imam Al-Shafi'i: For the Shafi'i position on Qunut in the last half of Ramadan and the preference for separating the Rak'ahs.
3. **Al-Hidayah** by Al-Marghinani: The premier text of Hanafi Fiqh detailing the obligation (Wujub) of Witr, its 3-Rak'ah structure, and the Takbeer before Qunut.
4. **Al-Mughni** by Ibn Qudamah (Hanbali): A comparative masterpiece that meticulously records the Hanbali preference for 1 Rak'ah, the year-round Qunut after Ruku', and Imam Ahmad's severe warning to those who abandon Witr.
5. **Sahih Bukhari & Sahih Muslim**: For the primary Hadith concerning the timing of Witr, the number of Rak'ahs, and the actions of the Prophet (ﷺ) when missing the night prayer.
6. **Sunan Abi Dawud & Sunan al-Tirmidhi**: For the Hadiths establishing the command to pray Witr and the differing narrations regarding the structure of the Rak'ahs.
