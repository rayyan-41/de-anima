# Leonhard Euler: The Master of Us All

Leonhard Euler (1707–1783) stands as arguably the most prolific and significant mathematician in history. His work was so foundational that it is often joked that mathematical discoveries are named after the first person to find them *after* Euler. He revolutionized nearly every branch of mathematics, from infinitesimal calculus to graph theory, and provided the modern notation for functions, logarithms, and trigonometry. His influence was so pervasive that [[Pierre-Simon Laplace]] famously remarked to his students, "Read Euler, read Euler, he is the master of us all."

- - -

## Act I: The Crucible

Born in Basel, Switzerland, on April 15, 1707, Leonhard Euler was the son of a pastor who intended for him to follow in the ministry. However, Euler’s early aptitude for mathematics brought him under the tutelage of [[Johann Bernoulli]], then the world’s foremost mathematician. While his father initially resisted, the influence of the Bernoulli family eventually secured Euler’s path into the sciences.

At the age of 13, Euler enrolled at the University of Basel, earning his Master’s degree by 16 with a dissertation comparing the philosophies of Descartes and Newton. In 1727, seeking greater opportunity than the small university town could offer, he accepted an invitation to the Imperial Russian Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg. This move was facilitated by [[Daniel Bernoulli]] and Nicolas Bernoulli, who had already established themselves there. It was in this cold, intellectually vibrant frontier that Euler began his meteoric rise, transitioning from a junior medical assistant role (his initial appointment) to a professor of physics and, eventually, the head of the mathematics department.

## Act II: The Zenith

By 1741, the political instability in Russia forced Euler to accept a call from [[Frederick the Great]] to join the Berlin Academy. Euler spent 25 years in Berlin, during which he wrote over 380 articles and several of his most influential books. Despite his immense productivity, his relationship with Frederick was strained; the monarch, who preferred the wit and literary flair of Voltaire, found Euler’s quiet, strictly mathematical focus to be dull, famously mocking him as the "mathematical Cyclops."

The "Cyclops" moniker referred to Euler’s deteriorating sight. In 1738, he nearly lost his right eye to a fever, a condition exacerbated by his intense work on cartography for the Russian government. Undeterred, Euler remarked, "Now I shall have fewer distractions." After returning to St. Petersburg in 1766 at the invitation of [[Catherine the Great]], a cataract in his left eye left him almost totally blind. Remarkably, his productivity did not decline. Blessed with a photographic memory and an "invincibility" of mental calculation, Euler dictated his findings to his sons and assistants, producing nearly half of his total body of work while completely blind. He was known to perform complex calculations in his head that his peers struggled to solve on paper.

## Act III: The Legacy

Euler’s second St. Petersburg period was a race against time. He survived a devastating fire in 1771 that destroyed his home; he was saved by his Swiss servant, Peter Grimm, and managed to salvage his manuscripts. He continued to work until his final day on September 18, 1783. While discussing the orbit of the newly discovered planet Uranus with his colleague Anders Johan Lexell, Euler suffered a brain hemorrhage. As his biographer famously put it, "He ceased to calculate and to live."

The scale of Euler’s output was so vast that the St. Petersburg Academy continued to publish his backlogged manuscripts for nearly 50 years after his death. His work laid the groundwork for [[Joseph-Louis Lagrange]] and inspired the analytical rigor of the 19th century.

## The Human Element

Euler was not merely a calculating machine; he was a man of deep composure and simple habits. He was a devoted father to 13 children (though only five survived to adulthood) and was known to write his most complex papers while holding a baby on one knee and surrounded by the chaos of a large household. 

His memory was legendary. He could recite the entire *Aeneid* of Virgil from memory, indicating even the first and last lines on every page of the edition he had read in his youth. This mental library allowed him to "see" the mathematics he could no longer view with his eyes. During the Great Fire of 1771, while his library burned, he remained remarkably calm, focusing on the preservation of the intellectual data rather than the physical loss.
