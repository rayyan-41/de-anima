### HIST - King Sigismund and the Control of Bohemia

## The Crusader Who Would Be Emperor

In the autumn of 1396, Sigismund of Luxembourg stood at what should have been his moment of triumph. At thirty-eight years old, he had assembled one of the largest crusading armies in a generation—perhaps 100,000 warriors—and marched to the fortress of Nicopolis on the Danube.

Instead, it became a catastrophe that would haunt him for the rest of his life.

The battle on September 25, 1396, resulted in the rout of the allied Crusader army at the hands of Ottoman forces under Sultan Bayezid I. The crusaders were riven by disputes of precedence. The young French nobles, particularly John of Nevers, insisted on leading the first charge. They were destroyed.

Sigismund barely escaped by ship down the Danube. If Nicopolis taught him anything, it was that idealism and chivalric fantasies were luxuries he could not afford. Power came through pragmatism.

### The Order of the Dragon: Politics Wrapped in Piety

On December 12, 1408, Sigismund and his wife, Barbara of Cilli, founded the Order of the Dragon. While it appeared to be a crusading order, it was actually a cunning tool for early political networking. It included both domestic and foreign nobles, binding together a coalition that could face down Ottomans and rebels alike. Among its members was Vlad II of Wallachia ("Dracul").

## Jan Hus: The Preacher Prague Could Not Silence

While Sigismund built his networks, Jan Hus was rising in Bohemia. Born around 1369-1372, Hus became a master at the University of Prague and leader of the Bethlehem Chapel. He preached in Czech, calling for reform and criticizing Church corruption. He was heavily influenced by John Wycliffe.

Hus's theology struck at the heart of Church power. He asserted that forgiveness came through repentance, not money (indulgences). The authorities moved against him, but with support from Bohemian nobles, his ministry continued.

## The Council of Constance: Where Empires and Souls Were Decided

In 1414, the Council of Constance gathered to resolve the Western Schism and address heresy. Sigismund needed the council to succeed to secure his imperial coronation. He invited Hus to attend under a "safe-conduct" guarantee.

It was a trap. Hus was arrested shortly after arriving. On July 6, 1415, Hus was burned at the stake for heresy. Sigismund broke his word.

## Bohemia Explodes: The First Defenestration

The news of Hus's execution reached Bohemia like a lit fuse. On July 30, 1419, the First Defenestration of Prague took place. Wenceslas IV died of shock, and Sigismund struggled to establish government as more than half of the Bohemian nobility refused to recognize him.

## The One-Eyed Prophet of War: Jan Žižka

Jan Žižka, a follower of Hus, became the leader of the Taborite armies. He was a military genius who invented armored "wagon forts" (mobile fortresses) to defeat professional cavalry.

At **Vítkov Hill** (1420), Žižka's small force defeated thousands of crusaders. At **Kutná Hora** (1421), despite being completely blind, Žižka pulled off his greatest tactical achievement, escaping encirclement and inflicting heavy losses on Sigismund's forces. Žižka died of plague in 1424, requested his skin be made into war drums.

## Sigismund's Long Road to Acceptance

After years of war, Sigismund was finally crowned Holy Roman Emperor in 1433. He negotiated the Basel Compacts, which allowed for religious choice in Bohemia—a rare concession of the time. He died in 1437 in Znojmo.

## Legacy

Sigismund remains a controversial figure. The Czechs never forgave him for Hus, seeing him as a symbol of imperial treachery. Yet, he was a tenacious survivor who managed to unite the Church and hold together three kingdoms under impossible constraints. The fires of Constance, however, seeded the coming Reformation.
