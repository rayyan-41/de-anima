## 5. The Maliki School's Position: The Primacy of the 'Amal of Madinah

The Maliki school, founded by the Imam of Dar al-Hijrah, Malik ibn Anas (d. 179 AH), presents one of the most fascinating and misunderstood epistemological frameworks regarding *Raf' al-Yadayn*. The dominant, established position (*mashhur*) of the Maliki school, as transmitted in *Al-Mudawwana al-Kubra* through Imam Malik's premier student Ibn al-Qasim, is that raising the hands during the transitions of the obligatory prayer (*fard*) is disliked (*makruh*). It is only recognized as a *Sunnah* at the opening *Takbirat al-Ihram*. (Some later Maliki scholars allow it in voluntary prayers (*nafl*), but the standard ruling for obligatory prayers remains abandonment).

### The Epistemological Foundation: 'Amal Ahl al-Madinah
To the untrained modern mind, which operates on a shallow, literalist reading of hadith collections, the Maliki position seems entirely contradictory. How could Imam Malik, the great compiler of *Al-Muwatta*, record the hadith of Ibn Umar affirming *Raf' al-Yadayn* in his own book, yet rule against it in his jurisprudence?

This apparent paradox is resolved by understanding Imam Malik’s profound legal theory: the Practice of the People of Madinah (*'Amal Ahl al-Madinah*). Imam Malik lived in the city of the Prophet (ﷺ) and was surrounded by the descendants of the Companions. For Malik, the continuous, unbroken, mass-transmitted practice of the thousands of scholars, pious worshippers, and descendants of the Companions living in Madinah was epistemologically superior to an isolated (*ahad*) hadith, even if that hadith possessed a flawless, authentic chain of transmission (*isnad*).

Imam Malik's reasoning is profoundly logical: The Prophet (ﷺ) prayed publicly five times a day in Madinah for ten years. If raising the hands at the transitions was his final, continuous, and legislated practice, the entire city of Madinah would have been doing it. It would have been inherited visually and practically from father to son, teacher to student. 

When Imam Malik looked at the congregations of Madinah—a city populated by the immediate successors of the Companions—he did not see the masses raising their hands at the transitions. Therefore, he concluded that while the hadith of Ibn Umar in his *Muwatta* was historically true (meaning the Prophet *did* do it at some point), it did not represent the final, normative *Sunnah* intended for continuity. The *'Amal* served as the ultimate filter to determine which prophetic actions were meant to be perpetual law and which were temporary or abrogated.

### The Mudawwana vs. The Muwatta
The text of *Al-Mudawwana* explicitly records Ibn al-Qasim asking Imam Malik about raising the hands when bowing and rising. Imam Malik replied: 

> "I do not know of this in the obligatory prayer. It is weak (*da'if*) to me, except in the beginning of the prayer, where he should raise his hands slightly."

When confronted with the hadith he himself recorded in the *Muwatta*, Imam Malik's position remained firm. A hadith is a transmission of a statement or an isolated event; the *'Amal* is the transmission of the living, breathing *Sunnah* of the entire Prophetic city. 

Some modern pseudo-scholars attempt to claim that the Maliki school "actually" supports *Raf' al-Yadayn* because some lesser transmissions from Malik (such as the transmission of Ibn Wahb or Ashhab) permit it. However, the indisputable, codified position of the Maliki *madhab*, upon which the scholars of North Africa, Andalusia, and West Africa relied for over a millennium, is the abandonment (*irsal*) of the hands. 

This highlights a crucial principle often lost in contemporary debates: the founder of a *madhab* is not bound by every authentic hadith he transmits. Fiqh is the science of weighing evidences. Imam Malik demonstrated supreme intellectual courage by prioritizing the lived reality of the Prophetic city over the textual reality of a solitary report, asserting that the *deen* is a living tradition, not merely a collection of texts.
