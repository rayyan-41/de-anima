## 4. The Hanafi School's Position: Abandonment Except at the Takbirat al-Ihram

The Hanafi school of jurisprudence, founded by Imam Abu Hanifa (d. 150 AH) in Kufa, represents the dominant legal tradition in South Asia, Turkey, Central Asia, and the Balkans. The official position (*mu'tamad*) of the Hanafi *madhab* is that *Raf' al-Yadayn* is only a *Sunnah* at the initial *Takbirat al-Ihram*. Raising the hands at the bowing (*ruku'*), rising from it, or standing for the third *rak'ah* is considered abrogated (*mansukh*) or abandoned (*matruk*), and performing it intentionally without belief in its obligation is viewed as *khilaf al-awla* (contrary to the preferred way) or slightly disliked (*makruh tanzihan*), though it emphatically does not invalidate the prayer.

### The Epistemological Foundation
To understand the Hanafi position, one must understand their approach to hadith and transmission. Imam Abu Hanifa operated in Kufa, a city that was established as a military and intellectual garrison by Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab. Umar dispatched Abdullah ibn Masud, one of the most senior, learned, and intimately close Companions of the Prophet (ﷺ), to teach the people of Kufa. The Hanafi school relies heavily on the inherited communal practice of Kufa, which they argue represents the final, settled practice of the Prophet (ﷺ) as transmitted by Ibn Masud and his elite students (like 'Alqamah and Ibrahim al-Nakha'i).

For the Hanafis, a widespread communal practice (*tawatur 'amali*) holds significant epistemic weight. If the Prophet (ﷺ) had continuously raised his hands throughout his life, the thousands of Companions who settled in Kufa would have transmitted this practice, and it would have become the standard there. Its absence in the continuous practice of the Kufan masses indicated to Abu Hanifa that the practice was eventually dropped by the Prophet (ﷺ).

### Primary Evidences

The cornerstone of the Hanafi argument is the narration of Abdullah ibn Masud (May Allah be pleased with him):

> 'Alqamah reports that Abdullah ibn Masud said: "Shall I not pray with you the prayer of the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ)?" He then prayed and did not raise his hands except the first time. 
> *(Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Sunan Abi Dawud, Sunan al-Nasa'i)*

Imam al-Tirmidhi categorized this hadith as *Hasan* (sound), and numerous scholars, including Ibn Hazm and the later Hanafi masters like 'Allamah Badr al-Din al-'Ayni and Anwar Shah al-Kashmiri, defended its authenticity robustly against detractors. The Hanafis argue that Ibn Masud, being an older Companion who accompanied the Prophet (ﷺ) on journeys and in his private quarters, would not have missed or forgotten a continuous physical action of the Prophet (ﷺ). If he did not raise his hands, it implies the Prophet (ﷺ) had ceased doing so in his final years.

A secondary line of evidence is the hadith of Al-Bara' ibn 'Azib (May Allah be pleased with him):

> "When the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) would begin the prayer, he would raise his hands near his ears, and he would not repeat it." 
> *(Sunan Abi Dawud)*

While some scholars of hadith (*muhaddithin*) criticized the addition "and he would not repeat it" (*la ya'ud*), Hanafi masters like Imam al-Tahawi (d. 321 AH) argued for its reliability based on multiple supporting chains and the practice of major successors (*Tabi'un*).

Furthermore, Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani (d. 189 AH), the brilliant student of Abu Hanifa, narrates in his *Muwatta* that the practice of the elite Companions in Kufa, including Ali ibn Abi Talib, was to refrain from raising the hands except at the opening. 

### The Concept of Abrogation (Naskh)
The Hanafi jurists do not deny the existence of authentic narrations (such as the hadith of Ibn Umar, which is in *Sahih al-Bukhari*) stating that the Prophet (ﷺ) raised his hands. Instead, they employ the jurisprudential mechanism of abrogation (*Naskh*). They argue that the prayer was instituted in stages. Initially, there was much movement permitted in prayer, which was gradually restricted to cultivate intense stillness and presence (*Khushu'*). 

The Hanafis cite the Qur'anic imperative: *"And stand before Allah with devout obedience [Qanitin]"* (Qur'an 2:238), and the hadith of the Prophet (ﷺ) where he saw people moving their hands in prayer and said: "Why do I see you raising your hands as if they were the tails of restive horses? Be tranquil in prayer" *(Sahih Muslim)*. Though Shafi'i scholars argue this specific hadith referred to raising hands during the final *Salam*, Hanafis draw a broader legal maxim (*'illah*) from it: the overarching trajectory of the Prophet's command was toward minimizing physical motion in the prayer. Thus, *Raf' al-Yadayn* was a valid early practice that was later abrogated in pursuit of perfect stillness (*sukun*).
