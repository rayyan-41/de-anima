## 6. The Shafi'i and Hanbali Schools: The Affirmation of the Sunnah

In stark contrast to the Kufan methodology of the Hanafis and the Madinan methodology of the Malikis, the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools derive their epistemology from the strict adherence to the explicit texts of authentic ahadith (*Nass*), particularly prioritizing the multiplicity and strength of chains over geographical communal practices. For both Imam Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i (d. 204 AH) and Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d. 241 AH), *Raf' al-Yadayn* is classified as a highly recommended Sunnah (*Sunnah Mu'akkadah*) at four specific junctures:
1. The opening *Takbir*.
2. Upon bowing for *Ruku'*.
3. Upon rising from *Ruku'*.
4. Upon standing up for the third *rak'ah* (from the first *tashahhud*).

### The Shafi'i and Hanbali Evidences

The foundational evidence for these two schools is the rigorously authenticated hadith of Abdullah ibn Umar (May Allah be pleased with him):

> "I saw the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) when he stood for the prayer, raising his hands until they were level with his shoulders. He did the same when he said the Takbir for bowing, and when he raised his head from bowing, he said 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' and did the same. And he did not do that in prostration (*sujud*)." 
> *(Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim)*

Imam al-Shafi'i, in his monumental work *Al-Umm*, and Imam al-Nawawi in *Al-Majmu'*, argue that this hadith is unambiguous, practically mass-transmitted (*mutawatir*) in meaning, and definitively establishes the Prophet's practice. Furthermore, they cite the hadith of Nafi' ibn Jubayr reporting from Ibn Umar that when the Prophet (ﷺ) stood from the two *rak'ahs*, he would raise his hands *(Sahih al-Bukhari)*.

They also rely heavily on the hadith of Abu Humayd al-Sa'idi (May Allah be pleased with him), who demonstrated the prayer of the Prophet (ﷺ) in the presence of ten of the senior Companions, explicitly including the raising of the hands at the transitions. Not a single Companion present contradicted his demonstration. For the Hanbalis and Shafi'is, this constitutes a tacit consensus (*ijma' sukuti*) among those Companions regarding the physical form of the prayer.

### The Epistemological Principle: The Affirmative Precedes the Negative

The Shafi'i and Hanbali jurists are fully aware of the Hanafi reliance on the hadith of Ibn Masud, where he did not raise his hands. How do they reconcile this? They employ a fundamental maxim in their *Usul al-Fiqh*: 

**"Al-muthbit muqaddam 'ala al-nafi"** *(The one who affirms takes precedence over the one who negates).*

If one truthful, reliable Companion (like Ibn Umar or Abu Humayd) explicitly affirms seeing the Prophet (ﷺ) perform an action, and another truthful, reliable Companion (like Ibn Masud) reports that he did not see it or did not do it, the affirmation is given precedence. The logic is that the one who affirms possesses surplus knowledge. Ibn Masud may have been absent, or he may have been relying on an earlier practice, whereas Ibn Umar and Abu Humayd possessed the knowledge of the *affirmation* of the act.

Furthermore, Imam al-Shafi'i profoundly challenged the Maliki reliance on *'Amal*. He argued that a clear, authentic Prophetic statement or action cannot be overridden by the practice of a city, because the people of the city are fallible and their practice over generations can drift, whereas the authentic text preserves the pristine Prophetic action. Imam al-Shafi'i famously stated: "When a hadith is found to be authentic, then that is my *madhab*."

Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal, the champion of the *Ahl al-Hadith*, viewed *Raf' al-Yadayn* as a hallmark of adhering closely to the Prophetic tradition. For the Hanbalis, abandoning *Raf' al-Yadayn* when one knows the authentic ahadith regarding it represents a deficiency in one's commitment to following the Prophet (ﷺ) completely. However, neither Imam al-Shafi'i nor Imam Ahmad ever claimed that omitting the raising of the hands invalidated the prayer. They recognized it purely as an issue of maximizing virtue (*fadilah*).
